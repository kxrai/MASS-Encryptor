Caesar Cipher

The Caesar Cipher is one of the simplest and most well-known encryption techniques. It involves shifting each letter in the plaintext by a fixed number of positions down the alphabet.

Usage: Commonly used for educational purposes to introduce encryption concepts.

Encoding Example: With a shift of 3, 'A' becomes 'D', 'B' becomes 'E', and so on.

Keyword Cipher

The Keyword Cipher uses a keyword to create a substitution alphabet. The keyword's letters are placed at the beginning of the alphabet, and the remaining letters follow in their usual order, excluding any letters already used in the keyword.

Usage: Useful for creating a simple yet more secure cipher than the Caesar Cipher.

Encoding Example: If the keyword is 'KEY', the substitution alphabet starts with 'KEYABCDFGH...'. 'A' becomes 'K', 'B' becomes 'E', etc.

Atbash Cipher

The Atbash Cipher is a simple substitution cipher where the alphabet is reversed. 'A' is substituted with 'Z', 'B' with 'Y', and so on.

Usage: Historically used in the Hebrew language, it is mainly of interest today for its historical significance.

Encoding Example: 'HELLO' becomes 'SVOOL' as 'H' maps to 'S', 'E' to 'V', etc.

Morse Code

Morse Code is a method used in telecommunication to encrypt text characters as sequences of dots and dashes. Each letter and number is represented by a unique combination of short and long signals.

Usage: Widely used in early radio communications and still in use for emergency signals.

Encoding Example: 'SOS' is encrypted as '... --- ...'.
